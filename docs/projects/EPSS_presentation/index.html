Background

  • This paper builds on an earlier paper (currently R+R, co-authored with Schafer/Schneider), which shows that political leaders’ illiberal language is congruent with institutional decline
  • However, existing literature as well as our previous paper do not unravel the causal mechanisms behind this relationship, i.e., how and when discursive shifts toward illiberal rhetoric lead to / go together with democratic decline
  • This paper aims to fill this gap, however, it is early stage with some first empirical results, but there are still several theoretical and measurement challenges to work through

Central Argument


Discursive shifts can occur without immediate institutional consequences


But when shifts become normalized…


They enable institutional erosion → congruent decline


Research Question: When do discursive shifts toward illiberal rhetoric become normalized, and how does this affect the institutional settings in democracies?

Defining Illiberal Discourse



Illiberal = Exclusion OR Concentration (or both)


Based on Maerz et al. (2026)

Two Dimensions Explained


Exclusion (vs. Inclusion)

  • Antagonism toward pluralism
  • Rejects minority rights
  • Dehumanizes out-groups

Concentration (vs. Dispersion)

  • Preference for unchecked power
  • Attacks courts, media, opposition
  • Rejects accountability


Captures populist, radical, and authoritarian rhetoric in one framework.


Based on Maerz et al. (2026)

Defining Normalization

Normalization = illiberal rhetoric becomes accepted as legitimate discourse


Three-stage causal sequence (overlapping):

Stage Definition
1. Repetition Single speakers consistently use illiberal language
2. Diffusion Other politicians imitate this illiberal language so it spreads from fringe to mainstream
3. Accommodation Contestation fails, not enough counter-speech, institutions don’t sanction

Central Hypothesis


Once normalization occurs, illiberal discourse and institutional erosion become mutually reinforcing — trajectories converge.


  • Before normalization: Discourse and institutions can diverge
  • After normalization: Congruent decline

Measuring Illiberal Language (Maerz et al., 2026)

Stage Input Process Output
Data collection Raw speeches Scraping, cleaning ~500k paragraphs
Labelling 10% sample LLM filtering, human coding Labelled data
DeBERTa models ~2400 labels/dimension Fine-tune DeBERTa (LoRA) DeBERTa filter + scores
Cross-validation Subset (500) GPT-5.2 scoring with quallmer LLM scores + audit
Validation DeBERTa/LLM scores Gold standard, manual checks Final scores


  • This is how we measure illiberal language on a scale of 0-10 (10 = most illiberal) in our paper on political leaders’ speeches (Maerz et al., 2026, currenltly R+R) based on 38,557 speeches from 452 political leaders in 123 countries (1973–2024)
  • In the current paper, I re-use and adjust this pipeline for an extended corpus which includes more actors, to track diffusion across elites.

Operationalizing Normalization


Indicator Measurement
Repetition Per speaker: mean illiberalism score (0–10), with a simple count of paragraphs they repeat on their own side
Diffusion Across speakers: number of speakers in the illiberal camp (mean ≥ 5) relative to the liberal camp (mean < 5), with each speaker’s share of paragraphs on their camp’s side as first indication of influence
Accommodation Across speeches per year: symmetry of the score distribution around the midpoint = contestation, illiberal skew = accommodation (counter-speech ratio and institutional sanctions: coming soon)


Normalized: High repetition + broad diffusion + low contestation

Resilient: Episodic rhetoric + confined + robust contestation

Current Challenges and Next Steps


  1. Theory: Concept of normalization, causal link between normalized illiberal language and democratic decline

  2. Measuring normalization: Tipping point vs. continuous saturation; levels of normalization; operationalization of diffusion and accomodation, e.g., weighing speakers in networks of power/influence? How to measure counter-speech / responses to illiberl speech? How to measure institutional sanctions?

  3. Measuring illiberal language: - If/How to adjust our existing DeBERTa models for new actors and countries?

  4. Empirical scope: - Focus only on these two cases and potentially drop the second part of the research question or include more cases and also explore the link to institutional decline?
